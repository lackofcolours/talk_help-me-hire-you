Recruitment process

What does recruitment process generally looks like?

From my POV, there are 6 stages

Phase 1: Preparing

  • Why hire an intern or junior talent?
  • Which team?
  • What does that team need?

‚úÖ Job description/ad

Organisation asks itself, why hire an intern or junior talent?

Depending on its size, business life cycle, and values, answers can range from:

  • Questionable "save cost"
  • Expanding rapidly, not enough talent in market
  • Train into niche specialities like ecommerce and Salesforce
  • Lofty "fostering future generation of coders"
  • (intern) Entice them into future role with us

This will inform job posting tones and attitudes.

Which team?

For org with multiple teams, sometimes teams can volunteer or request.

Team's culture and natuer of work will inform the "What you'll learn" section from the ad.

Team needs

  • Team is full of disgruntled old people and keen on fresh young energy
  • Members' growth plans involve practice mentoring
  • Team practice/refine onboarding process
  • Team has regular/rotation spot for junior talent
  • (intern) Temporary help to get over a feature release

Informs "What you'll do" section

Phase 3: Screening

  • Reactive - Candidates apply
  • Proactive - Search on a platform
  • Both

Goal: 20-100 candidates per role

2,000 SoT profiles

1 week to screen ‚âà 57 candidates / hour üò±

3 main ways

Well known companies with track record will adopt a reactive strategy. Just screen from people who applied.

So if you never registered interest or reached out, you won't be considered.

Lesser known companies understand that if candidates don't know about them, no one would apply.

So they'd actively reach out, and shortlist candidates.

Organisations with a bit of dedicated resource (perosonnel, time, and $$) may adopt both

What is my goal during this phase? Manageable ratio of around 20-100 per 1 role

Say there are 2,000 candidates on SoT

Say you're given 1 week (35hrs) to narrow down the pool for teams.

If I'm a proactive type, I'll be trying to sort through 57 profiles per hour

Screening tools

  • SoT filters
    (student status, dev, and etc.)
  • SoT completed profiles
  • CV/resume
  • Portfolio or Git repos

‚úÖ Narrowed down to 20-100 candidates

What tools do I have at my disposal?

Student status (penultimate, last year, etc) filter, filled out profiles, well written CV keyword which matched the job description/team needs, and spreadsheets.

Empty or no effort profile, CV, and lack of portfolio could be deadly

Due to time and resource constraints, candidates become numbers and spreadsheets

After a week's of hard work, I now have a much smaller list to present to my teams

Phase 4: Selecting

  • Most resource heavy - Require many people's time
  • Most intense period - Short, and very busy
    • Shortlist candidates (fine tooth comb)
    • Technical assessment (sometimes)
    • Interviews

At this point, you students have applied to 10 companies, or may have heard from a few

...is waiting for a response or call for next steps, and posting nervous slack messages to eachother.

Employer's side - things gets real

Up until now, there might be only couple people working recruitment.

This is where teams hosting talent gets involved

From pool of candidates, filter who they want

Which means, if you're late, and missed cut off period (not always annouced), you're less likely to be looked at

This is also the most intense and busy period.

Tricky to schedule so many people in. So be mindful of employer's time and avoid rescheduling.

Shortlist for assessment/interview

  • SoT profile (effort and attitude)
  • CV/Resume (personal statement and links to works)

‚úÖ Shortlisted 6-10 candidates

No longer a number's game

As someone shortlisting candidates, I look for

  • Articulate profile, clearly highlighting career aspirations
  • Good attitude can't be spelled out. Don't just say I love learning, or working with people. Back it up with side project or activities?

CV/resume

  • What you said in your profile and resume match the nature of the role. Love UX but applied for data science?
  • Done works and listed out? Great! Where is it? Portfolio, links?
  • Portfolio isn't required, but greatly boost you
  • Not going to look at more than 3. Ain't no body got time
  • Knows git? Real bonus. Hard to teach at work. Show and link it.
  • Deployed work - Bonus you know how to deploy!
  • One very well completed and deployed GitHub project is as good as any
  • Last year I wanted to give candidates a chance, and downloaded their github work to run locally. Some is broken and doesn't run
  • If don't know how to deploy work but it is on GitHub? GitHub Pages is free.
  • Work isn't web based and can't be deployed for free? Upload video of how it works. Saves me the installation
  • You don't say you love C#, no mention of HTML CSS or JS, when applying for a front end web role.
    No one wants to be someone's backup role
  • Waitressed in your past life? Fab, list our human skills.
  • Hosted events or organised functions? Tell me more. This demonstrate organisation skills
  • Make yo-self memerable

At this point, I've looked at 25-100 candidates... I'm tired. Difference may be so minute

Technical assessment

Doesn't always happen

Usually when the recruitment is higher stake or is competitive

  • Online (HackerRank, CodeSubmit, and etc.)
  • Take-home assessment
  • White boarding (during interview)

For example, last year in one of the teams, people think having a junior may reduce productivity.

So we had to test if candidate has a basic understanding of git, HTML, and CSS.

For some roles, we had top 20 candidates who all have side projects, do extracurricular activities and seem to have very similar uni experience.

Online

I personally hate online assessment. Especially the timed pressure!

But it the most efficient thing we have to filter through many candidates, so I don't put too much weight on it.

Tip 1: A lot of these record answer everytime you hit run code. Use this instead of paper. It's like maths test showing thoughts process

Tip 2: A lot of these tests are free for candidates. Practice away. Again, like maths class

Take home

If given take home assessment, read instructions very carefully.

If possible, go above and beyond to show your prowess.

For example: Got emailed question to solve. Instead of submitting by attaching folder via email, do the whole thing on Git and deploy!

Use README.md file to your advantage. Explain your choices and decision if you were choosing between 2 possible methods.

White-board

Interviewer ask question, you answer

Talk through thought process. Ask clarfying questions. Gather more data to complete work!

Interview

  • SoT speed interview
  • Full interview
  • Grad day/Open day

Speed interview

  • Classic SoT 10mins speed interview
  • Less time consuming for employers
  • Most stressful for both students and employers
  • Giving concise but complete answers
  • How well does the candidate present themselves?
  • What do they value most?
  • What's their working style
  • Why are they interested in the role?

Full interview

Often used for roles that will likely turn into full time, or the org's first junior role.

Generally 1 or 1.5 hours

For both interview types, prepare questions to ask employers about the team

Grad day/Open day

  • Come into office to do a bunch of activities with other candidates, Hunger Games style
  • Assess team work
  • Test flexibility
  • Observe reactions/attitudes

Deciding who to offer

  • Not a time waster
  • Semi-autonomous learner
  • Empathetic communicator
  • Collaborator
  • Enrich team's culture (not 'fit' into team)
  • Team can offer valuable skill
  • Likely to have a good time

‚úÖ Ranked 3-6 candidates to give an offer

Lets say I have a candidate with stellar performance in technical assessment

I'd choose one who makes me want to work with them during interviews

I'm hiring a team mate.

As mentioned, everyone in this process are busy people. Did the candidate respect our time by being punctual?

Will they need much handholding on the job? Or will they be able to pick up things as they go with minor mentorship

Especially in a multicultural team, will candidate be able to work effectively with people of differing comm styles?

Are they likely to respectfully/pragmatically raise issues or ask for help?

Ahh, the detestable 'team fit'

When hiring for people who are the same as us, we'll create monoculture.

Very thing which resulted in discrimination and lack of diversity.

No, we're looking for someone who could help shift the scale a little. But not too disruptive

If the team is full of introvert, I'd be weary of adding yet another introvert, or an extreme extrovert in which could create too much conflict.

Want to create healthy tension between styles that will help elevate the team.

What can WE offer candiate?

If the candidate already knows all tech stack of the team, wouldn't this be a waste of their time?

Are they likely to be happy with the team, and with the company?

Internship is a form of a PR activity, and investment in future reputation

Phase 5: Hiring

Most preferred/top ranked candidate gets offer via SoT

If offer refused or not accepted in 24hrs,
next one in the ranking gets it

If ono one accepts, start all over

If you're one of the shortlisted candidates on offer list, well done

It means you've impressed us enough to make it there.

Frankly, I would be happy with any of the top candidates. But I had to rank and choose

Chances are, there was a minute difference between you

This is where the details of your work, and our interactions matter. It sets you apart
